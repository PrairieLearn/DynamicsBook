Moments of inertia

Moment of inertia about axis $\hat{a}$ through point $P$.

\[I_{P,\hat{a}} = \int_\mathcal{B} \rho r^2 \,dV\]

Here $r$ is the perpendicular distance from the axis through $P$ in direction $\hat{a}$.

Moments of inertia about coordinate axes through point $P$.

\[\begin{aligned} I_{P,\hat\imath} &= \iiint_\mathcal{B} \rho (y^2 + z^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \\ I_{P,\hat\jmath} &= \iiint_\mathcal{B} \rho (z^2 + x^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \\ I_{P,\hat{k}} &= \iiint_\mathcal{B} \rho (x^2 + y^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \end{aligned}\]

Here $(x,y,z)$ are the coordinates in the body measured from point $P$.

Example Problem: Moment of inertia of a square plate.

A solid square uniform plate has mass $m$, side-length $\ell$, and thickness $h$. What is the moment of inertia about the axis through the center of mass $C$ in the perpendicular direction $\hat{k}$?

We will use the coordinate formula #rem-ec. To do this, we measure the position from the point $C$ about which we are computing the moment of inertia, so the coordinate origin is at $C$. The figure to the right shows the 3D configuration of the body, where we see that the $x$-coordinate range of the body is $-\ell/2$ to $\ell/2$, and the same for the $y$-coordinate, while the $z$-coordinate ranges from $-h/2$ to $h/2$.

Taking the density of the plate to be $\rho$, we thus have: \[\begin{aligned} I_{C,\hat{k}} &= \iiint_\mathcal{B} \rho (x^2 + y^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \int_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2} \int_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2} \rho (x^2 + y^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \int_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2} \left[ \rho \left(\frac{1}{3}x^3 + y^2 x\right)\right]_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2} \,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \int_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2} \rho \left(\frac{1}{12}\ell^3 + y^2 \ell \right) \,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \left[ \rho \left(\frac{1}{12}\ell^3 y + \frac{1}{3} y^3 \ell \right) \right]_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2} \,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \rho \left(\frac{1}{12}\ell^4 + \frac{1}{12} \ell^4 \right) \,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \rho \frac{1}{6}\ell^4 \,dz \\ &= \left[ \rho \frac{1}{6}\ell^4 z \right]_{-h/2}^{h/2} \\ &= \rho \frac{1}{6}\ell^4 h. \end{aligned}\] The total mass of the plate is $m = \rho \ell^2 h$, so we can write the moment of inertia as \[I_{C,\hat{k}} = \frac{1}{6} m \ell^2.\]

Transforming and combining moments of inertia

Parallel axis theorem.

\[I_{P,\hat{a}} = I_{C,\hat{a}} + m \, {r_{CP}}^2\]

Warning: Parallel axis theorem must start from center of mass $C$.

Example Problem: Moment of inertia of a square plate about a corner.

A solid uniform square plate has mass $m$, side-length $\ell$, and thickness $h$. What is the moment of inertia about the axis through the corner $P$ in the perpendicular direction $\hat{k}$?

We can re-use our solution to Example Problem #rem-xs, where we computed the moment of inertia of a square place about the center to be $I_{C,\hat{k}} = \frac{1}{6} m \ell^2$. The parallel axis theorem #rem-el now gives: \[\begin{aligned} I_{P,\hat{k}} &= I_{C,\hat{k}} + m \, {r_{CP}}^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{6} m \ell^2 + m \left( \left(\frac{\ell}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\ell}{2}\right)^2 \right) \\ &= \frac{2}{3} m \ell^2. \end{aligned}\]

Alternatively, we can compute the corner moment of inertia directly by integrating with formula #rem-ec. To do this, we put the coordinate origin at the point $P$ about which we wish to find the moment of inertia, as shown in 3D to the right. From this we see that the $x$ range is $0$ to $\ell$, the same for the $y$ range, and the $z$ range is $-h/2$ to $h/2$.

The integral formula now gives: \[\begin{aligned} I_{P,\hat{k}} &= \iiint_\mathcal{B} \rho (x^2 + y^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \int_0^{\ell} \int_{0}^{\ell} \rho (x^2 + y^2) \,dx\,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \int_{0}^{\ell} \left[ \rho \left(\frac{1}{3}x^3 + y^2 x\right)\right]_{0}^{\ell} \,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \int_{0}^{\ell} \rho \left(\frac{1}{3}\ell^3 + y^2 \ell \right) \,dy\,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \left[ \rho \left(\frac{1}{3}\ell^3 y + \frac{1}{3} y^3 \ell \right) \right]_{0}^{\ell} \,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \rho \left(\frac{1}{3}\ell^4 + \frac{1}{3} \ell^4 \right) \,dz \\ &= \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \rho \frac{2}{3}\ell^4 \,dz \\ &= \left[ \rho \frac{2}{3}\ell^4 z \right]_{-h/2}^{h/2} \\ &= \rho \frac{2}{3}\ell^4 h. \end{aligned}\] The total mass of the plate is $m = \rho \ell^2 h$, so we can write the final expression for the moment of inertia is \[I_{P,\hat{k}} = \frac{2}{3} m \ell^2.\] This is the same as the expression we obtained above using the parallel axis theorem, but clearly the parallel axis theorem version is much easier.

Adding moments of inertia.

\[I_{P,\hat{a}} = I^{\mathcal{B}_1}_{P,\hat{a}} + I^{\mathcal{B}_2}_{P,\hat{a}}\]

Example Problem: Moment of inertia of an L-shaped plate.

A solid uniform L-shaped plate has mass $m$ and dimensions as shown. What is the moment of inertia about the axis through the corner $P$ in the perpendicular direction $\hat{k}$?

We can re-use our solution to Example Problem #rem-xs, where we computed the moment of inertia of a square place about the center to be $I_{C,\hat{k}} = \frac{1}{6} m \ell^2$, together with parallel axis theorem #rem-el and the additive theorem #rem-ea.

To do this, we must first realize that the L-shape can be decomposed into four square plates, as shown. Each small square body has the same mass and the same moment of inertia about its center, so: \[\begin{aligned} m_1 &= \frac{1}{4} m \\ I^{\mathcal{B}_1}_{C_1,\hat{k}} &= \frac{1}{6} m_1 (2d)^2 = \frac{1}{6} \left(\frac{1}{4} m\right) 4d^2 = \frac{1}{6} m d^2, \end{aligned}\] and similarly for each other small square body.

Now individual moments of inertia about the corner $P$ can be found using the parallel axis theorem: \[\begin{aligned} I^{\mathcal{B}_1}_{P,\hat{k}} &= I^{\mathcal{B}_1}_{P,\hat{k}} + m_1 \, {r_{C_1P}}^2 = \frac{1}{6} m d^2 + \left(\frac{1}{4} m\right) \left(d^2 + (3d)^2\right) = \frac{8}{3} m d^2 \\ I^{\mathcal{B}_2}_{P,\hat{k}} &= I^{\mathcal{B}_2}_{P,\hat{k}} + m_2 \, {r_{C_2P}}^2 = \frac{1}{6} m d^2 + \left(\frac{1}{4} m\right) \left(d^2 + d^2\right) = \frac{2}{3} m d^2 \\ I^{\mathcal{B}_3}_{P,\hat{k}} &= I^{\mathcal{B}_1}_{P,\hat{k}} = \frac{8}{3} m d^2 \\ I^{\mathcal{B}_4}_{P,\hat{k}} &= I^{\mathcal{B}_4}_{P,\hat{k}} + m_4 \, {r_{C_4P}}^2 = \frac{1}{6} m d^2 + \left(\frac{1}{4} m\right) \left((5d)^2 + d^2\right) = \frac{20}{3} m d^2. \end{aligned}\] Here we observed that body $\mathcal{B}_3$ will have the same moment of inertia as $\mathcal{B}_1$, saving some computation.

Combining the individual moments of inertia now gives us the total: \[\begin{aligned} I_{P,\hat{k}} &= I^{\mathcal{B}_1}_{P,\hat{k}} + I^{\mathcal{B}_2}_{P,\hat{k}} + I^{\mathcal{B}_3}_{P,\hat{k}} + I^{\mathcal{B}_4}_{P,\hat{k}} \\ &= \frac{8}{3} m d^2 + \frac{2}{3} m d^2 + \frac{8}{3} m d^2 + \frac{20}{3} m d^2\\ &= \frac{38}{3} m d^2. \end{aligned}\] We could also directly compute the total moment of inertia using the integral formula #rem-ec, which would be quite complex.

Basic shapes

Point mass: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_{P,\hat{k}} &= m r^2 \end{aligned}\]

Rectangular prism: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_{C,\hat\imath} &= \frac{1}{12} m ({\ell_y}^2 + {\ell_z}^2) \\ I_{C,\hat\jmath} &= \frac{1}{12} m ({\ell_z}^2 + {\ell_x}^2) \\ I_{C,\hat{k}} &= \frac{1}{12} m ({\ell_x}^2 + {\ell_y}^2) \end{aligned}\]

Cylindrical thick shell: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_{C,\hat\imath} &= I_{C,\hat\jmath} = \frac{1}{12} m (3({r_1}^2 + {r_2}^2) + {\ell_z}^2) \\ I_{C,\hat{k}} &= \frac{1}{2} m ({r_1}^2 + {r_2}^2) \end{aligned}\]

Spherical thick shell: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_C &= \frac{2}{5} m \left(\frac{{r_2}^5 - {r_1}^5}{{r_2}^3 - {r_1}^3}\right) \end{aligned}\]

Simplified shapes

Rod: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_{C,\hat{k}} &= \frac{1}{12} m \ell^2 \\ I_{P,\hat{k}} &= \frac{1}{3} m \ell^2 \end{aligned}\]

Solid cylinder or disk: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_{C,\hat{k}} &= \frac{1}{2} m r^2 \end{aligned}\]

Hollow cylinder or hoop: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_{C,\hat{k}} &= m r^2 \end{aligned}\]

Solid ball: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_C &= \frac{2}{5} m r^2 \end{aligned}\]

Hollow sphere: moments of inertia

\[\begin{aligned} I_C &= \frac{2}{3} m r^2 \end{aligned}\]
