Calculus and vectors

Time-dependent vectors can be differentiated in exactly the same way that we differentiate scalar functions. For a time-dependent vector \(\boldsymbol{v}(t)\), the derivative \(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}(t)\) is

Vector derivative definition

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{\boldsymbol{v}}(t) &= \frac{d}{dt} \boldsymbol{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\boldsymbol{v}(t + \Delta t) - \boldsymbol{v}(t)}{\Delta t}\end{aligned}\]

Example: Vector derivatives.

Show a vector \(\boldsymbol{v}(t)\) with a fixed origin and the end tracing out a curved path (path is shown as a dotted line). Show \(\boldsymbol{v}(t)\) animated. Then show \(\boldsymbol{v}(t + \Delta t)\) and the construction for the derivative, resulting in the derivative vector.

Notation: We will use either the dot notation \(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}(t)\) or the full derivative notation \(\frac{d\boldsymbol{v}(t)}{dt}\), depending on which is clearer and more convenient. We will often not write the time dependency explicitly, so we might write just \(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}\) or \(\frac{d\boldsymbol{v}}{dt}\).

In a fixed basis we differentiate a vector by differentiating each component:

Vector derivative in components

\[\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}(t) = \dot{v}_1(t) \,\hat{\imath} + \dot{v}_2(t) \,\hat{\jmath} + \dot{v}_3(t) \,\hat{k}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{v}(t) &= v_1(t) \,\hat{\imath} + v_2(t) \,\hat{\jmath} \\ \dot{\boldsymbol{v}}(t) &= \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\boldsymbol{v}(t + \Delta t) - \boldsymbol{v}(t)}{\Delta t} \\ &= \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{(v_1(t + \Delta t) \,\hat{\imath} + v_2(t + \Delta t) \,\hat{\jmath}) - (v_1(t) \,\hat{\imath} + v_2(t) \,\hat{\jmath})}{\Delta t} \\ &= \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{(v_1(t + \Delta t) - v_1(t)) \,\hat{\imath} + (v_2(t + \Delta t) - v_2(t)) \,\hat{\jmath}}{\Delta t} \\ &= \left(\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v_1(t + \Delta t) - v_1(t)}{\Delta t} \right) \,\hat{\imath} + \left(\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{v_2(t + \Delta t) - v_2(t) }{\Delta t}\right) \,\hat{\jmath} \\ &= \dot{v}_1(t) \,\hat{\imath} + \dot{v}_2(t) \,\hat{\jmath}\end{aligned}\]

We can also differentiate vector expressions, such as dot products and cross products. These act like multiplication, so the product rule applies:

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt}(\boldsymbol{v} \cdot \boldsymbol{u}) &= \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \boldsymbol{u} + \boldsymbol{v} \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{u}} \\ \frac{d}{dt}(\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{u}) &= \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \times \boldsymbol{u} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \dot{\boldsymbol{u}}\end{aligned}\]

Example: Differentiating complex vector expressions.

What is \(\frac{d}{dt} \big( \boldsymbol{u} \cdot (\boldsymbol{v} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{w}) \big)\)?

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt} \big( \boldsymbol{u} \cdot (\boldsymbol{v} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{w}) \big) &= \dot{\boldsymbol{u}} \cdot (\boldsymbol{v} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{w}) + \boldsymbol{u} \cdot \frac{d}{dt} (\boldsymbol{v} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{w}) \\ &= \dot{\boldsymbol{u}} \cdot (\boldsymbol{v} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{w}) + \boldsymbol{u} \cdot (\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} + \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \times \boldsymbol{w} + \boldsymbol{v} \times \dot{\boldsymbol{w}})\end{aligned}\]

We can break a vector derivative down into the change in length and the change in direction:

\[\begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{v} &= v \hat{v} \\ \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} &= \dot{v} \hat{v} + v \dot{\hat{v}}\end{aligned}\]

To compute the derivative of length and direction we can use:

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{v} &= \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \hat{v} \\ \hat{v} &= \frac{1}{v} \operatorname{Comp}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \hat{v})\end{aligned}\]

This means that

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{v} \hat{v} &= (\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \hat{v}) \hat{v} = \operatorname{Proj}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \boldsymbol{v}) \\ v \dot{\hat{v}} &= v \frac{1}{v} \operatorname{Comp}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \hat{v}) = \operatorname{Comp}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \hat{v})\end{aligned}\]

So the decomposition is

Vector derivative decomposition

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} &= \underbrace{\dot{v} \hat{v}}_{\operatorname{Proj}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \boldsymbol{v})} + \underbrace{v \dot{\hat{v}}}_{\operatorname{Comp}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \hat{v})}\end{aligned}\]

For length:

\[\begin{aligned} v &= \sqrt{\boldsymbol{v} \cdot \boldsymbol{v}} \\ \frac{d}{dt} v &= \frac{d}{dt} \big( (\boldsymbol{v} \cdot \boldsymbol{v})^{1/2} \big) \\ \dot{v} &= \frac{1}{2} (\boldsymbol{v} \cdot \boldsymbol{v})^{-1/2} (\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \boldsymbol{v} + \boldsymbol{v} \cdot \dot{\boldsymbol{v}}) \\ &= \frac{1}{2\sqrt{v^2}} (2 \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \boldsymbol{v}) \\ &= \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \hat{v}\end{aligned}\]

For direction:

\[\begin{aligned} \hat{v} &= \frac{\boldsymbol{v}}{v} \\ \frac{d}{dt} \hat{v} &= \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\boldsymbol{v}}{v}\right) \\ \dot{\hat{v}} &= \frac{\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} v - \boldsymbol{v} \dot{v}}{v^2} \\ &= \frac{\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}}{v} - \frac{\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \hat{v}}{v^2} \boldsymbol{v} \\ &= \frac{1}{v} \big( \dot{\boldsymbol{v}} - (\dot{\boldsymbol{v}} \cdot \hat{v}) \hat{v} \big)\\ &= \frac{1}{v} \operatorname{Comp}(\dot{\boldsymbol{v}}, \hat{v})\end{aligned}\]
