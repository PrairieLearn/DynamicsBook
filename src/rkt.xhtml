Tangential/normal basis

Consider a particle moving with position vector $\vec{r}$ and corresponding velocity $\vec{v}$ and acceleration $\vec{a}$. The tangential/normal basis $\hat{e}_t,\hat{e}_n,\hat{e}_b$ is:

Tangential/normal basis vectors.

\[\begin{aligned} \hat{e}_t &= \hat{v} & &\text{tangential basis vector} \\ \hat{e}_n &= \frac{\dot{\hat{e}}_t}{\|\dot{\hat{e}}_t\|} = \frac{\operatorname{Comp}(\vec{a},\vec{v})}{\|\operatorname{Comp}(\vec{a},\vec{v})\|} & &\text{normal basis vector} \\ \hat{e}_b &= \hat{e}_t \times \hat{e}_n & &\text{binormal basis vector} \\ \end{aligned}\]

The tangential basis vector $\hat{e}_t$ points tangential to the path, the normal basis vector $\hat{e}_n$ points perpendicular (normal) to the path towards the instantaneous center of curvature, and the binormal basis vector $\hat{e}_b$ completes the right-handed basis.

Tangential/normal basis associated with movement around a curve in 3D. Observe that the velocity $\vec{v}$ is always in the $\hat{e}_t$ direction and that the acceleration $\vec{a}$ always lies in the $\hat{e}_t,\hat{e}_n$ plane (the osculating plane). The center of curvature and osculating circle are defined below.

Path length

The distance along the path is $s$, which can be found by integrating the speed $v$ with respect to time. Correspondingly, the speed is the derivative of path length.

Path length $s$ and speed $v$.

\[\begin{aligned} v &= \dot{s} \\ s &= \int_0^t v \,dt \end{aligned}\]

When differentiating geometric quantities related to paths, it is often convenient to differentiate with respect to path length rather than time. These derivatives are related by:

Derivatives with respect to path length $s$ and time $t$.

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt} &= v \frac{d}{ds} \\ \frac{d}{ds} &= \frac{1}{v} \frac{d}{dt} \end{aligned}\]

This is a statement of the chain rule. For example, the first equation is: \[\frac{d}{dt} = \frac{ds}{dt} \frac{d}{dt} = \dot{s} \frac{d}{dt} = v \frac{d}{dt}.\] The second equation is just a simple re-arrangement.

Curvature, torsion, and osculating circles

Curvature and torsion.

\[\begin{aligned} \kappa &= \frac{d\hat{e}_t}{ds} \cdot \hat{e}_n = \frac{1}{v} \dot{\hat{e}}_t \cdot \hat{e}_n & &\text{curvature} \\ \rho &= \frac{1}{\kappa} & &\text{radius of curvature} \\ \tau &= -\frac{d\hat{e}_b}{ds} \cdot \hat{e}_n = -\frac{1}{v} \dot{\hat{e}}_b \cdot \hat{e}_n & &\text{torsion} \\ \sigma &= \frac{1}{\tau} & &\text{radius of torsion} \end{aligned}\]

The osculating circle is the equivalent circular path. That is, a particle traveling on the osculating circle at the same location and speed as our particle would have matching velocity and acceleration vectors.

Osculating circle.

\[\text{center $C = \vec{r} + \rho \hat{e}_n$,} \qquad \text{radius $\rho$,} \qquad \text{normal $\hat{e}_b$}\]

Prove that this matches $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{a}$.

Kinematic relations

Angular velocity of the tangential/normal basis.

\[\begin{aligned} \vec{\omega} &= v\tau \,\hat{e}_t + v \kappa \,\hat{e}_b \end{aligned}\]

Tangential/normal basis vector derivatives.

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{\hat{e}}_t &= \phantom{-v\kappa\,\hat{e}_t + } v\kappa\,\hat{e}_n \\ \dot{\hat{e}}_n &= -v\kappa\,\hat{e}_t \phantom{ + v\kappa\,\hat{e}_n + } + v\tau\,\hat{e}_b \\ \dot{\hat{e}}_b &=\phantom{-v\kappa\,\hat{e}_t + } v\tau\,\hat{e}_n \end{aligned}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{\hat{e}}_t &= \dot{\hat{v}} \\ &= \vec{\omega} \times \hat{e}_t \\ &= (\omega_t \,\hat{e}_t + \omega_b \,\hat{e}_b) \times \hat{e}_t \\ &= \omega_b \,\hat{e}_n \\ &= \frac{v}{\rho} \hat{e}_n \end{aligned}\]

Velocity and acceleration in tangential/normal basis.

\[\begin{aligned} \vec{v} &= \dot{s} \, \hat{e}_t \\ \vec{a} &= \ddot{s} \, \hat{e}_t + \frac{\dot{s}^2}{\rho} \hat{e}_n \end{aligned}\]

Velocity and acceleration in the tangential/normal basis. Note that the tangential/normal basis does not depend on the choice of origin or the position vector, in contrast to the polar basis.

The tangential/normal basis is also called the Frenet–Serret frame after Jean Frédéric Frenet and Joseph Alfred Serret, who discovered it independently around 1850. The equations #rkt-ed for the basis derivatives are often called the Frenet-Serret formulas, typically written in terms of $s$ derivatives: \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d\hat{e}}{ds} &= \phantom{-\kappa\,\hat{e}_t + } \kappa\,\hat{e}_n \\ \frac{d\hat{e}}{ds} &= -\kappa\,\hat{e}_t \phantom{ + \kappa\,\hat{e}_n + } + \tau\,\hat{e}_b \\ \frac{d\hat{e}}{ds} &=\phantom{-\kappa\,\hat{e}_t + } \tau\,\hat{e}_n. \end{aligned}\]

If we divide the angular velocity vector #rkt-ew by $v$ then we obtain the vector \[\frac{1}{v} \vec{\omega} = \tau\,\hat{e}_t + \kappa\,\hat{e}_b,\] which is known as the Darboux vector after its discoverer, Jean Gaston Darboux.

Curves and curvature

Curvature of parametric curve $\vec{r}(a)$ in 3D.

\[\kappa = \frac{\|\vec{r}' \times \vec{r}''\|}{\|\vec{r}'\|^3}\]

Curvature of parametric curve $(x(a),y(a))$ in 2D.

\[\kappa = \frac{|x'y'' - y'x''|}{(x'^2 + y'^2)^{3/2}}\]
