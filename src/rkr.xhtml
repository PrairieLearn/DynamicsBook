Rotations and Angular Velocity

A rotation of a vector is a change which only alters the direction, not the length, of a vector. A rotation consists of a rotation axis and a rotation rate. By taking the rotation axis as a direction and the rotation rate as a length, we can write the rotation as a vector, known as the angular velocity vector \(\vec{\omega}\). We use the right-hand-rule to describe the direction of rotation.

Show a cloud of vectors rotating in 3D around an angular velocity vector. Maybe have the angular velocity vector change in length, showing the rotation slowing down and speeding up. Maybe also have it change in direction, showing the rotation axis change.

In 2D the angular velocity can be thought of as a scalar (positive for counter-clockwise, negative for clockwise). This scalar is just the out-of-plane component of the full angular velocity vector. We can draw the angular velocity as either a vector pointing out of the plane, or as a circle-arrow in the plane, which is simpler for 2D diagrams.

If a vector \(\vec{a}\) is rotating with angular velocity vector \(\vec{\omega}\), the rate of change of \(\vec{a}\) is

Rate of change of a rotating vector

\[\dot{\vec{a}} = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{a}\]

We will see below that this is correct.

Properties of rotations

  • Vector derivatives due to rotation are orthogonal to the vector:

    \[\begin{aligned} \dot{\vec{a}} \cdot \vec{a} = 0 \end{aligned}\]

    Derivation: Using the scalar triple product formula:

    \[\begin{aligned} \vec{a} \cdot \dot{\vec{a}} &= \vec{a} \cdot \big( \vec{\omega} \times \vec{a} \big) &= \vec{\omega} \cdot \big( \vec{a} \times \vec{a} \big) &= 0 \end{aligned}\]

  • If we have two vectors \(\vec{b}\) and \(\vec{a}\) both rotating with an angular velocity \(\vec{\omega}\), then \(\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}\) is constant:

    \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt} \big( \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} \big) = 0 \end{aligned}\]

    Derivation: Using the scalar triple product formula:

    \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt} \big( \vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} \big) &= \dot{\vec{b}} \cdot \vec{a} + \vec{b} \cdot \dot{\vec{a}} \\ &= (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{a} + \vec{b} \cdot (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{a}) \\ &= \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{b}) + \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{\omega}) \\ &= \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{b}) - \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{\omega} \times \vec{b}) \\ &= 0 \end{aligned}\]

  • Lengths of vectors are preserved by rotation:

    \[\begin{aligned} a = \sqrt{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a}} = \text{constant} \end{aligned}\]

    Derivation: Use derivative of dot product formula.

  • Angles between vectors are preserved by rotation:

    \[\begin{aligned} \theta = \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a}}{b a}\right) = \text{constant} \end{aligned}\]

    Derivation: Use derivative of dot product formula.

  • If \(\vec{a}\) is parallel to \(\vec{\omega}\) then \(\dot{\vec{a}} = 0\).

This shows that the rotation formula \(\dot{\vec{a}} = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{a}\) does indeed perform a rigid rotation about \(\vec{\omega}\).

The units of \(\vec{\omega}\) are \(\rm rad/s\).

Decompositions of rotation rates

\[\begin{aligned} \vec{\omega} = \frac{\dot{\vec{a}} \times \vec{a}}{a^2} = \frac{\dot{\vec{a}} \times \vec{a}}{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a}}\end{aligned}\]

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt} \,\hat{a} &= \vec{\omega} \times \,\hat{a} \\ \frac{d}{dt} \vec{a} &= \vec{\omega} \times \vec{a} \\ \frac{d}{dt} \vec{a} &= \dot{a} \,\hat{a} + \vec{\omega} \times \vec{a}\end{aligned}\]

Rodrigues’ rotation formula

Rodrigues’ rotation formula: \(\vec{a}\) is rotated by angle \(\theta\) about \(\hat{b}\) to give:

Rodrigues’ rotation formula

\[\operatorname{Rot}_{\theta,\hat{b}}(\vec{a}) = \vec{a} \cos\theta + (\hat{b} \times \vec{a}) \sin\theta + \hat{b} (\hat{b} \cdot \vec{a}) (1 - \cos\theta)\]

Assume \(\vec{a}\) is not parallel to \(\hat{b}\). Then let \(\vec{b} = \hat{b} \times \vec{a}\) and \(\vec{a} = \vec{b} \times \vec{b}\), so \(\hat{a}, \hat{b}, \hat{b}\) is a right-handed orthonormal basis (see PV.CC). Take \(\phi\) to be the angle between \(\vec{a}\) and \(\hat{b}\). Then we do a rotation by \(\theta\) in the \(\hat{a}\)-\(\hat{b}\) plane:

\[\begin{aligned} \vec{a} &= a \sin\phi \,\hat{a} + a \cos\phi \,\hat{b} \\ \operatorname{Rot}_{\theta,\hat{b}}(\vec{a}) &= a \cos\theta \sin\phi \,\hat{a} + a \sin\theta \sin\phi \,\hat{b} + a \cos\phi \,\hat{b}\end{aligned}\]

Now we want to convert from the \(\hat{a},\hat{b},\hat{b}\) basis to write the rotated result in terms of \(\vec{a}, (\hat{b} \times \vec{a}), \hat{b}\). To do this, we need to work out what \(\hat{a},\hat{b},\hat{b}\) are in terms of these other vectors.

\[\begin{aligned} \hat{b} &= \frac{\hat{b} \times \vec{a}}{\|\hat{b} \times \vec{a}\|} = \frac{\hat{b} \times \vec{a}}{a \sin\phi} \\ \hat{a} &= \frac{\vec{b} \times \hat{b}}{\|\vec{b} \times \vec{b}\|} = \frac{\vec{b} \times \hat{b}}{a \sin\phi} = \frac{(\hat{b} \times \vec{a}) \times \hat{b}}{a \sin\phi} = \frac{\hat{b} \times (\vec{a} \times \hat{b})}{a \sin\phi} \\ &= \frac{\vec{a} - (\hat{b} \cdot \vec{a}) \hat{b}}{a \sin\phi} = \frac{1}{a \sin\phi} \vec{a} - \frac{\hat{b} \cdot \vec{a}}{a \sin\phi} \hat{b}\end{aligned}\]

Substituting these into the rotated vector expression above gives

\[\begin{aligned} \operatorname{Rot}_{\theta,\hat{b}}(\vec{a}) &= a \cos\theta \sin\phi \left( \frac{1}{a \sin\phi} \vec{a} - \frac{\hat{b} \cdot \vec{a}}{a \sin\phi} \hat{b} \right) \\ &\qquad+ a \sin\theta \sin\phi \left( \frac{\hat{b} \times \vec{a}}{a \sin\phi} \right) + a \cos\phi \,\hat{b} \\ &= \cos\theta \,\vec{a} - \cos\theta \,(\hat{b} \cdot \vec{a}) \,\hat{b} + \sin\theta \,(\hat{b} \times \vec{a}) + a\cos\theta \,\hat{b} \\ &= \cos\theta \,\vec{a} + (1 - \cos\theta) (\hat{b} \cdot \vec{a}) \,\hat{b} + \sin\theta \,(\hat{b} \times \vec{a})\end{aligned}\]

Example: Rotations are not vectors.

Rotations are not vectors. We could write a rotation about the direction \(\hat{b}\) by angle \(\theta\) as the vector \(\vec{a} = \theta \hat{b}\). But this would be dangerous, as adding rotation vectors would not correspond to composing the rotations (as rotations do not commute). See p. 89 in GCP.
